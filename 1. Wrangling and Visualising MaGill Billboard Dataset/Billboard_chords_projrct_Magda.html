1. Introduction

The purpose of this project is to present an insight into a dataset designed for musicological studies. We use the McGill Billboard Database, a systematically sampled and professionally curated collection of chord transcriptions of harmony in pop songs presented by Burgoyne et al. (2011).

The McGill Billboard chords data

The McGill Billboard Data is a result of the PhD thesis “Stochastic processes and database-driven musicology” published in 2012 by John Ashley Burgoyne at the McGill University Montréal. Burgoyne (2012) presents a corpus of the most played chords from more than 1000 pop songs from the latter half of the twentieth century (1958-1991), as ranked by Billboard magazine’s Hot 100 chart. Billboard Magazine has published weekly since 4th August 1958 the top 100 pop singles available for sale in the United States. A substantial change to the methodology for computing the Hot 100 in 1991 by the Billboard (Burgoyne, 2012) determines the November 1991 as the end date of the dataset. This cut of the dataset has been necessary to maintain a uniform sampling procedure.

It has to be noted that the randomly selected Billboard tracks in the McGill database represent only the very most popular songs loccally limited to the US. Therefore we should not draw conclusions about music in general based only on a sample included in this data.

The most common chords in the McGill Billboard

A chord is a kind of a simultaneity of notes, heard as some sort of unit. A chord is is a basic component of harmony, a fundamental element of music beside the rhythm, dymanic, timbre and texture. The kind of chords which artists use in their songs may be a signature feature of a music genere or even of an artist. Beside the differences how the chords sound, some chords are easier to play than other on particular instruments. Because the McGill Billboard data consists of the pop genere, we can check if there is a difference in the chords played, depending on the instrument. But at first we can answer a basic question: which chords more commonly played than others? The table below show the first ten chords are major chords, followed by A and E minor. The top ten McGill Billboard chords occur in the outermost circle of fifths, which is a geometrical representation of the relationship among the 12 pitch classes of the chromatic scale. Hence, it is more interesting to display the most common chords in a circle than just a bar chart. Caution: the chart is a circle of fifths, the percentages do not sum to 100%. The most played minor chord is A minor with 1.7%.
